# Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

<Callout type="info" emoji="🔔">
The Normal distribution, often called the **"bell curve,"** is the single most important probability distribution in statistics. It appears everywhere in nature and forms the backbone of machine learning, hypothesis testing, and inferential statistics.
</Callout>

## Core Concepts

If a random variable $X$ follows a Normal distribution, we write:
$$
X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)
$$

Where:
*   $\mu$ (mu) is the **mean** (which dictates the center of the bell).
*   $\sigma^2$ (sigma squared) is the **variance** (which dictates the spread). The square root, $\sigma$, is the standard deviation.

### Probability Density Function (PDF)

<Callout type="error" emoji="🤯">
Don't panic! The equation below looks terrifying, but it perfectly defines the elegant, smooth shape of the bell curve.
</Callout>

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}
$$

While complex, let's break down why this works mathematically:
*   The $e^{-(\dots)^2}$ part creates the exponential decay that forms the "bell" shape.
*   The $\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$ part centers the curve at $\mu$ and scales its width by $\sigma$.
*   The $\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}$ part is a normalizing constant. It ensures that the total area under the entire curve equals exactly $1.0$.

## The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7)
One of the most useful heuristics in statistics applies to all Normal distributions:

*   <span style={{color: '#10b981', fontWeight: 'bold'}}>$\approx 68.27\%$</span> of data falls within **1 standard deviation** ($\pm 1\sigma$) of the mean.
*   <span style={{color: '#3b82f6', fontWeight: 'bold'}}>$\approx 95.45\%$</span> of data falls within **2 standard deviations** ($\pm 2\sigma$) of the mean.
*   <span style={{color: '#8b5cf6', fontWeight: 'bold'}}>$\approx 99.73\%$</span> of data falls within **3 standard deviations** ($\pm 3\sigma$) of the mean.

## Real-World Examples
The Normal distribution is everywhere because of the *Central Limit Theorem* (covered later).
1.  **Human Heights:** The heights of adult males in a country cluster around a mean and taper off symmetrically.
2.  **Measurement Error:** The tiny errors a machine makes when precisely measuring weights or lengths.
3.  **Test Scores:** Standardized tests like the SAT or GRE are explicitly designed to force scores into a normal distribution.

## Test Your Knowledge

<MathExample title="Example: Normal Curve Empirical Rule">
  <MathQuestion>
    A population of adult male heights is normally distributed with a mean $\mu = 70$ inches and a standard deviation $\sigma = 3$ inches. What percentage of men are between 64 inches and 76 inches tall?
  </MathQuestion>
  <MathSolution>
    Notice that 64 inches is exactly $\mu - 2\sigma$ ($70 - 2(3) = 64$).
    Notice that 76 inches is exactly $\mu + 2\sigma$ ($70 + 2(3) = 76$).
    
    According to the Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7), approximately **95%** of normally distributed data falls within 2 standard deviations of the mean.
  </MathSolution>
</MathExample>
