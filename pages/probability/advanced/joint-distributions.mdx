# Joint Distributions & Covariance

<Callout type="info" emoji="👯">
Until now, we have mostly dealt with one random variable $X$ at a time. But in the real world, variables rarely exist in isolation. They interact, influence each other, and dance together in high-dimensional space.
</Callout>

## The Joint PDF and PMF

The **Joint Distribution** $P(X, Y)$ tells us the probability of seeing both $X$ and $Y$ happen simultaneously.

*   **For Discrete variables:** $P(X=x, Y=y)$ is the probability that both outcomes occur.
*   **For Continuous variables:** $f(X, Y)$ is the probability density of a point in 2D space. To find the probability of a region, we use double integration: $\iint f(x, y) \, dx \, dy$.

### Independence
If two variables have no relationship, their joint distribution is just the product of their individuals:
$$ P(X, Y) = P(X) \cdot P(Y) $$

---

## Marginalization: "Summing Out"

If you have a Joint distribution $P(X, Y)$ but you only want to know about $X$, you can "marginalize" $Y$ by summing (or integrating) it out:

$$ P(X) = \sum_{y \in Y} P(X, Y) $$

This is the equivalent of looking at a 3D landscape and flattening it onto one of its axes to see the 2D profile.

---

## Covariance: The Math of Interaction

While [Correlation](./correlation) is a convenient number between -1 and 1, **Covariance** is the underlying "raw" measurement of how much two variables change together.

$$ \text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - \mu_x)(Y - \mu_y)] $$

*   **Positive Covariance:** If $X$ is above its mean, $Y$ tends to be above its mean too.
*   **Negative Covariance:** If $X$ is above its mean, $Y$ tends to be below its mean.
*   **Zero Covariance:** No linear relationship.

### The Covariance Matrix: The God-Tier Structure
In Machine Learning, we don't just compare two variables. We compare dozens. We use a **Covariance Matrix** ($\Sigma$), where every entry $(i, j)$ is the covariance between variable $i$ and variable $j$.

The diagonal of this matrix is just the **Variance** of each individual variable!

## Test Your Knowledge

<MathExample title="Example: Marginalizing a Joint Distribution">
  <MathQuestion>
    Suppose you have two discrete variables $X$ and $Y$ with the following joint distribution:
    - $P(X=1, Y=1) = 0.2$
    - $P(X=1, Y=2) = 0.3$
    - $P(X=2, Y=1) = 0.1$
    - $P(X=2, Y=2) = 0.4$
    Find the marginal probability $P(X=1)$.
  </MathQuestion>
  <MathSolution>
    To find $P(X=1)$, we sum the joint probabilities across all possible values of $Y$:
    $P(X=1) = P(X=1, Y=1) + P(X=1, Y=2)$
    $P(X=1) = 0.2 + 0.3 = 0.5$
    
    Similarly, $P(X=2) = 0.1 + 0.4 = 0.5$.
  </MathSolution>
</MathExample>
