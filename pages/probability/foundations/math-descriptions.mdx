# The Mathematical Descriptions

<Callout type="info" emoji="📐">
To work with probability distributions, statisticians have developed three core mathematical functions. Depending on whether your random variable is **discrete** (counting) or **continuous** (measuring), you will use different functions.
</Callout>

## 1. Probability Mass Function (PMF)

<Callout type="default" emoji="📊">
**Used for:** Discrete Random Variables.
</Callout>

The PMF tells you the exact probability that a discrete random variable is **exactly equal** to some value. Because the variable is discrete, you can think of the PMF as assigning a "mass" of probability to specific, isolated points.

**Notation:**
$$
P(X = x) = p(x)
$$

**Rules:**
1.  Every individual probability must be between 0 and 1: $0 \le p(x) \le 1$
2.  The sum of all probabilities across all possible outcomes must equal exactly 1: $\sum p(x) = 1$

*Example: The PMF of rolling a fair 6-sided die is $p(x) = 1/6$ for $x \in \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$.*

---

## 2. Probability Density Function (PDF)

<Callout type="error" emoji="🌊">
**Used for:** Continuous Random Variables.
**Warning:** The PDF does *not* output a direct probability!
</Callout>

The PDF is the continuous equivalent of the PMF, but it requires a paradigm shift. For a continuous variable (like exact human height), the probability of someone being *exactly* 180.00000000... cm tall is technically zero! 

Instead of looking at exact points, the PDF describes the probability **density**. To find an actual probability, you must calculate the **area under the curve** over a specific range using calculus (integration).

**Notation:**
$$
f(x)
$$

**To find the probability of falling between $a$ and $b$:**
$$
P(a \le X \le b) = \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx
$$

**Rules:**
1.  Density can never be negative: $f(x) \ge 0$
2.  The total area under the entire curve must equal exactly 1: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) dx = 1$

---

## 3. Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)

<Callout type="warning" emoji="📈">
**Used for:** Both Discrete and Continuous Random Variables.
</Callout>

The CDF is arguably the most practical function. It answers the question: "What is the probability that my variable will be **less than or equal to** a specific value $x$?"

It is the running total (cumulative sum or integral) of the probabilities up to that point.

**Notation:**
$$
F(x) = P(X \le x)
$$

**Rules:**
1.  It always starts at 0: $\lim_{x \to -\infty} F(x) = 0$
2.  It always ends at 1: $\lim_{x \to \infty} F(x) = 1$
3.  It is monotonically non-decreasing (it never goes down, it only goes up or stays flat).

## Test Your Knowledge

<MathExample title="Example: PDF vs CDF">
  <MathQuestion>
    Let the continuous random variable $X$ have a probability density function (PDF) $f(x) = 2x$ for $0 \le x \le 1$. 
    Find the cumulative distribution function (CDF) $F(x)$ and calculate $P(X \le 0.5)$.
  </MathQuestion>
  <MathSolution>
    The CDF $F(x)$ is the integral of the PDF from the lower bound to $x$:
    
    $F(x) = \int_{0}^{x} 2t \, dt = \left[ t^2 \right]_0^x = x^2$
    
    To find the probability $P(X \le 0.5)$, we just plug $0.5$ into the CDF:
    $F(0.5) = (0.5)^2 = 0.25$
    
    There is a 25% probability that $X$ is less than or equal to 0.5.
  </MathSolution>
</MathExample>
